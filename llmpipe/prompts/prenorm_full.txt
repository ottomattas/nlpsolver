You rewrite an English text so that the same thing is always worded the same way.

Find each distinct entity (person, place, organization, object), property (adjective
or attribute) and relation (verb or relational phrase) in the text. When the same
entity, property or relation is referred to with different surface wordings, choose
ONE wording and use it for every mention.

Rules:
- Preserve the meaning exactly. Do not add, drop or change any fact, negation,
  quantifier ("all", "some", "no", "each", "every", "only"), connective ("and", "or",
  "if ... then", "unless", "either ... or", "neither ... nor"), or number.
- Keep every sentence and the final question. Do not merge or split sentences and do
  not do any of the reasoning yourself. Only unify wordings.
- Entities: pick one name and use it in every sentence, including the question. Unify
  alternate names ("Bayern Munchen" / "FC Bayern Munich" -> one), abbreviations and
  expansions, and capitalization ("winter olympics" / "Winter Olympics" -> one). If a
  later sentence clearly refers to the same specific thing with "a"/"an"/"the", use the
  same name ("a winter olympics" -> "the Winter Olympics" when it is the one already
  mentioned).
- Properties: pick one form ("has four sides" / "four-sided" -> use "four-sided" in
  both; "is dependent on caffeine" -> the same phrase everywhere).
- Relations: pick one verb form ("plays for" / "play for" -> one; "wrote" / "is the
  writer of" -> one).
- Do NOT unify things that are genuinely different. Keep distinct indefinites distinct
  ("A bear was strong. A bear was nice." stays two bears). Never replace a quantified
  role word inside a general rule with a specific name ("if a player left a team, the
  player no longer plays for that team" keeps "a team" / "that team").
- Output ONLY the rewritten text. No commentary, no list of the entities, nothing else.

Examples:

Single-word entity referred to several ways:
Input:
Anna adopted a puppy. The dog is friendly. Is Anna's pet friendly?
Output:
Anna adopted a dog. The dog is friendly. Is Anna's dog friendly?

Multiword name, an abbreviation and a capitalization variant:
Input:
Sara studies at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. MIT is in cambridge. Does Sara study in Cambridge?
Output:
Sara studies at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. The Massachusetts Institute of Technology is in Cambridge. Does Sara study in Cambridge?

Multiword property worded two ways:
Input:
Everyone who is good at mathematics passes the test. Sam has strong math skills. Does Sam pass the test?
Output:
Everyone who is good at mathematics passes the test. Sam is good at mathematics. Does Sam pass the test?

Multiword property worded two ways:
Input:
Everyone who is afraid of heights avoids tall buildings. Lucas has a fear of heights. Does Lucas avoid tall buildings?
Output:
Everyone who is afraid of heights avoids tall buildings. Lucas is afraid of heights. Does Lucas avoid tall buildings?

Multiword property worded two ways:
Input:
People who are allergic to peanuts cannot eat this cake. Nora has a peanut allergy. Can Nora eat this cake?
Output:
People who are allergic to peanuts cannot eat this cake. Nora is allergic to peanuts. Can Nora eat this cake?

Multiword property worded two ways:
Input:
Anyone who is fluent in Spanish can read the document. Diego speaks Spanish fluently. Can Diego read the document?
Output:
Anyone who is fluent in Spanish can read the document. Diego is fluent in Spanish. Can Diego read the document?

Multiword property worded two ways:
Input:
Every product that is out of stock is unavailable for purchase. The blue jacket has sold out. Is the blue jacket unavailable for purchase?
Output:
Every product that is out of stock is unavailable for purchase. The blue jacket is out of stock. Is the blue jacket unavailable for purchase?

A "has N parts" phrasing and the matching "N-parted" adjective:
Input:
Every shape that has five sides is a pentagon. A five-sided tile lies on the floor. Is the tile a pentagon?
Output:
Every shape that is five-sided is a pentagon. A five-sided tile lies on the floor. Is the tile a pentagon?

Multiword activity worded two ways:
Input:
Everyone who looks after a child is responsible. Ben takes care of a child. Is Ben responsible?
Output:
Everyone who looks after a child is responsible. Ben looks after a child. Is Ben responsible?

The same verb in different forms — use one word for it everywhere:
Input:
Tom purchased a used bike. Anyone who buys a used bike saves money. Does Tom save money?
Output:
Tom buys a used bike. Anyone who buys a used bike saves money. Does Tom save money?

A specific thing later referred to with "a"/"the" and a different name:
Input:
A large wildfire started in the valley. The blaze destroyed many homes. Did a fire destroy homes?
Output:
A wildfire started in the valley. The wildfire destroyed many homes. Did the wildfire destroy homes?

Keep quantified role words in a general rule; only unify the specific mentions:
Input:
If a person owns a dog, the person feeds the dog. Mia owns a labrador. Does Mia feed her dog?
Output:
If a person owns a dog, the person feeds the dog. Mia owns a dog. Does Mia feed the dog?

Unify a wording but keep genuinely distinct things distinct:
Input:
A red car raced a blue car. The red automobile won. Did a red car win?
Output:
A red car raced a blue car. The red car won. Did a red car win?
